### SECTION 2: THE DEEP DIVE — Buying From Flippers: What They Fix, What They Skip, and How to Negotiate

Roughly 1 in 5 listings under $200K in Temple right now is LLC-owned. That means a flipper bought it, rehabbed it, and is trying to sell it for a margin. Some of these are excellent buys. Some are lipstick on a foundation crack. Here's how to tell the difference.

**What flippers almost always fix (because it photographs well):**
- Kitchen cabinets and countertops
- Bathroom tile and fixtures
- Flooring (usually LVP — cheap, durable, photographs like hardwood)
- Paint inside and out
- Light fixtures and ceiling fans
- Landscaping and curb appeal

This is the stuff that shows up in MLS photos. It's what moves the needle from $80K as-is to $145K "fully remodeled."

**What flippers frequently skip (because it's invisible and expensive):**
- Foundation repair — if the house "needs piers," that's $8-15K the flipper would rather not spend. Some do it. Many level the floors cosmetically and move on.
- Plumbing under the house — galvanized or cast iron pipes in a 1950s home are a ticking clock. Re-piping is $4-8K and nobody sees it in the listing photos.
- Electrical panel upgrades — a 100-amp panel on a home with new HVAC drawing 40+ amps is a fire risk. Upgrading to 200-amp is $2-4K.
- Roof decking — new shingles over rotting decking is a 5-year problem. The roof "looks" new.
- Insulation — especially in pier and beam crawlspaces. Out of sight, out of mind.
- Sewer line — camera scope it. A collapsed sewer line is a $5-10K surprise.

**How to evaluate a flip — three questions that filter 90% of the risk:**

1. **Pull the permit history.** If the flipper pulled permits for electrical, plumbing, and HVAC, they did it right. If they only pulled a "remodel" permit or nothing at all, the mechanical work may not be to code. Temple's permit records are public.

2. **Check the foundation independently.** Don't rely on the listing agent saying "foundation is fine." Spend $350-500 on a structural engineer's report. On a pier and beam home, get under the house yourself. Look at the piers, joists, and subfloor.

3. **Compare the all-in cost.** If a flipper bought for $80K and is selling for $145K, that's a $65K spread. Rehab probably cost $30-40K. Their margin target is $20-30K. If you can verify the rehab quality is solid, you're still buying below replacement cost — purchase + rehab + holding costs + your time would run you more than $145K.

**How to negotiate with a flipper:**

Flippers are not emotional sellers. They care about IRR. Every month that property sits vacant is eating their margin. A flipper with a property listed 30+ days is doing mental math on margin lost per week.

Your strongest tools:
- **Fast close.** Offer 14-21 day close with proof of funds or pre-approval. Speed beats price for most flippers.
- **As-is with inspection contingency.** Signals you're serious but protects you.
- **Price reduction data.** "You listed at $159K, cut to $145K after 30 days. You're now at 45 DOM. I'll close in 14 days at $133K." That's a conversation most flippers will have.

The deal in this week's autopsy — 1807 S 11th — is a textbook example. LLC-owned, price reduced $14,900, sitting vacant, concessions offered.
