## 1. What Is a Physician Mortgage Loan

A physician mortgage (also called a "doctor loan" or "white coat mortgage") is a specialty loan product designed specifically for medical professionals. It is NOT a conventional conforming loan — it does not follow standard Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac underwriting rules.

**Why it exists:** Banks know doctors are statistically low-risk borrowers despite carrying massive student debt and having low income during training. The average medical school graduate carries $223,130 in education debt (Class of 2025, up 5% YoY). 70% of medical school graduates have six-figure education debt. Yet physician default rates are among the lowest of any profession. Banks use physician loans as a marketing tool to acquire high-income clients early in their careers.

**How it works:**
- Lender offers 0-10% down payment with NO private mortgage insurance (PMI)
- Student loan debt is treated more favorably in debt-to-income (DTI) calculations
- Employment contracts/offer letters accepted instead of pay stubs or W-2 history
- Higher loan limits than conforming loans ($1M-$2.5M depending on lender)
- Primary residence only — no investment properties, no second homes

## 3. Key Benefits

### Benefit #1: Zero or Low Down Payment
- 0% down available up to $750K-$1M (lender dependent)
- 5% down available up to $1.5M
- 10% down available up to $2M-$2.5M

### Benefit #2: No PMI — Ever
This is the big one. Conventional loans with <20% down require PMI, which costs 0.5%-1.5% of the loan annually. On a $300K loan with 5% down, that is $125-$375/month in PMI that physician loan borrowers simply do not pay.

### Benefit #3: Student Loan Treatment
Conventional lenders calculate student loan DTI using 1% of total balance (or the fully amortized payment). A $250K student loan balance = $2,500/month calculated debt under conventional rules. Physician lenders use your actual IBR/IDR payment — often $0-$300/month during residency. This single difference can increase purchasing power by $100K-$200K.

### Benefit #4: Employment Contract Acceptance
Most conventional lenders require 2 years of W-2 employment history. Physician loan programs accept a signed employment contract with a start date within 60-120 days of closing. A PGY-1 resident can close on a home before their first paycheck using their match letter.

### Benefit #5: High Loan Limits
Conforming loan limit in 2026: $806,500 (most of Texas). Physician loans routinely go to $1M-$2M+ without jumbo loan pricing penalties.

## 4. Student Loan Treatment — The Game-Changer

This is the section that makes or breaks a resident's ability to buy a home.

### Conventional Loan Rules (Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac):
- If loans are on IDR/IBR with a $0 payment: lender uses **1% of total balance** as monthly debt
- $250,000 in student loans = **$2,500/month** calculated debt
- This alone can disqualify a resident earning $71K/year

### FHA Rules:
- Uses 1% of outstanding balance OR actual payment, whichever is greater
- Only slightly better than conventional

### Physician Loan Rules:
- Uses your **actual monthly IDR/IBR payment** (even if $0)
- Some lenders: if loans deferred 12+ months from closing, they can be **excluded entirely**
- DTI maximum: up to **50%** (vs. 43-45% conventional)

### Real Example:
- Resident salary: $71,000/year ($5,917/month gross)
- Student loan balance: $250,000 on IBR
- IBR payment during residency: $250/month
- Conventional lender sees: $2,500/month student debt → DTI blown, loan denied
- Physician lender sees: $250/month student debt → loan approved

## 9. The Catch — What They Don't Tell You

### Catch #1: You're Paying a Rate Premium
Physician loans carry rates 0.25%-0.75% higher than what you'd get on a conventional loan with 20% down. Over 30 years on a $300K loan, that is $18,000-$54,000 in extra interest. If you have the ability to put 20% down, a conventional loan is almost always cheaper.

### Catch #2: Variable Rate Traps
Many physician loans are offered as ARMs (5/6 or 7/6 adjustable rate mortgages) because the initial rate is lower and more attractive. The trap: if you don't sell or refinance before the adjustment period, your payment can increase 10%-300%. A $2,000/month payment could become $2,600+ overnight.

**When ARM makes sense:** Resident buying for 3-5 years during training, plans to sell/move for attending position. 5/6 ARM saves $200-$400/month vs. fixed = $12,000-$24,000 over 5 years.

**When ARM is dangerous:** Attending settling into a permanent home. Always go 30-year fixed.

### Catch #3: Underwater Risk at 0% Down
With zero equity, any market dip puts you underwater. If Temple home values dropped 5% (they've already softened 4.2% YoY as of Feb 2026), a $300K purchase is now worth $285K — you owe more than it's worth. You can't sell without bringing cash to closing.

### Catch #4: The "Doctor House Trap"
Banks will approve physicians for WAY more than they should spend. A PGY-5 making $81K can get approved for $500K+. But a $500K mortgage payment on $81K salary = house-poor. The math:
- $500K at 6.75%, 0% down = ~$3,243/month P&I
- Property tax (~2.16%): $900/month
- Insurance: $200/month
- **Total: ~$4,343/month = 64% of gross income**
- Anything above 28-33% of gross is financially dangerous

### Catch #5: Primary Residence Only
Cannot use physician loans for investment properties, rentals, or second homes (except Alliant at 90% LTV). This is strictly for where you live.

### Catch #6: Lenders Come and Go
Physician loans are marketing tools. Lenders enter and exit the market based on strategy and economics. A lender offering great terms today may discontinue their program next year.

### Catch #7: Jumbo Territory Without Jumbo Awareness
Physician loans above $806,500 (2026 conforming limit) are technically jumbo territory. Some physician loan programs don't price these as jumbo, but the risk profile is jumbo — larger loan, potentially less equity, higher total cost.

## 15. PSLF Strategy (BSW Is a Non-Profit)

### The Hidden Advantage of BSW Temple:

Baylor Scott & White Health is a **501(c)(3) non-profit organization**. This means every year of residency/fellowship at BSW Temple counts toward Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF).

**PSLF Requirements:**
1. 120 qualifying monthly payments (10 years)
2. While employed full-time by a qualifying non-profit employer
3. Under an income-driven repayment plan (IDR/IBR/PAYE)
4. Direct federal loans only

**How This Connects to Physician Mortgage Strategy:**

- A 5-year surgical resident at BSW Temple makes 60 of 120 required PSLF payments during training
- During residency, IBR payments are LOW ($0-$300/month on $71K salary with family size adjustments)
- If they take an attending position at another non-profit hospital, they complete the remaining 60 payments
- After 10 years total: remaining student loan balance is **forgiven tax-free**

**Strategy:** Keep student loan payments as LOW as possible during residency (IBR), use the physician mortgage to build equity in a home, let PSLF handle the student debt. This is the optimal financial play for residents at BSW Temple.

**Warning:** If a physician goes into private practice or a for-profit hospital after residency, PSLF payments during residency are wasted. The strategy only works if the full 10-year plan is at non-profit employers.
