## THE BIG PICTURE: What Agents Won't Say

### Texas Does NOT Regulate Home Builders
- Texas is one of 21 states that **does not require a state license for general contractors or home builders**
- TREC (Texas Real Estate Commission) **has no jurisdiction over builders**
- From 2003-2009, the Texas Residential Construction Commission (TRCC) required builder registration and continuing education, provided an inexpensive dispute resolution process, and mandated express warranties — **the Texas Legislature sunsetted it in 2009**
- Anyone can legally advertise and perform general contracting work across Texas without a state-issued license
- The public inspector only checks that a home is built to code (safe to live in) — **not that it is well-constructed**

### The 120-Violation Average
- Texas home inspectors find **an average of 120 flagrant building code violations on each new house inspected**
- This does NOT include the even more common failure to follow manufacturers' installation instructions
- Root cause: speed of construction due to builder competition + low-skill subcontractors working for rock-bottom prices

### The Arbitration Trap
- D.R. Horton and Lennar contracts include **mandatory arbitration clauses** requiring buyers to waive their right to go to court
- Consumer advocates say the private arbitration system **systematically favors the builder**
- South Carolina Supreme Court ruled DR Horton's arbitration clause "unconscionable" and "unenforceable" (2016)
- Same court reached the same conclusion for Lennar's clause (2022)
- **In Texas, a 2023 court ruling EXPANDED arbitration** — even family members and subsequent owners may be bound

### 32% Regret Rate
- 32% of new construction homeowners regret their purchase (national data)

## CENTRAL TEXAS-SPECIFIC ISSUES

### Temple's Expansive Clay Soil Problem

**The Science:**
- Temple sits on complex soil profiles with extensive **expansive clay** that swells when wet and contracts during dry spells
- Expansive soils can expand **up to 30% in volume** when absorbing water
- Generates pressures up to **15,000 PSF** on foundations
- Hot, dry summers cause clay to shrink dramatically, leaving **voids under homes**
- Heavy rains cause rapid swelling, pushing pressure back against the foundation
- This constant expansion/contraction cycle is THE major cause of foundation movement in the area

**The Temple-Specific Trap:**
- Many Temple homes sit on a **combination of clay AND rock**
- One section of foundation on firm rock, another on shifting clay
- Result: tilted or sinking foundations, especially where soil prep was insufficient
- **After record-breaking heat in 2023, many Central Texas homeowners saw cracks zigzag across walls and slabs as parched ground pulled away from foundations**

**Post-Tension Slab Issues:**
- Most new construction in Central Texas uses post-tension slabs
- Cables should be stressed 7-10 days from pour — many builders wait 10+ days
- Cracks often form before tensioning; once cracked, they can't be eliminated
- Texas cycles of extreme dryness and sudden rain put extra stress on PT slabs
- Risk of cracking, deflection, and internal cable failure

**What Buyers Should Do:**
- Hire an **independent** inspector (not the builder's inspector)
- Specifically request a structural engineer experienced with post-tension systems
- Ask about soil testing/geotechnical report for the lot
- Maintain consistent moisture around the foundation (soaker hoses in summer)

## THE MUD/PID TAX TRAP

### What Builders Don't Explain

**MUD (Municipal Utility District):**
- Funds roads, drainage, sewer, water infrastructure in new developments
- Bonds last **20-40 years** — paid through property taxes
- MUD tax DOES go down over time as bonds are paid off
- But for the first 20+ years, adds significantly to monthly payment

**PID (Public Improvement District):**
- Funds community enhancements: roads, water, landscaping, parks
- Assessments are **separate from property taxes** but appear on your tax bill
- Can last **20-40 years**
- Can be paid upfront to avoid interest charges (most buyers don't know this)

**The Dollar Impact:**
- **$1,000–$4,000+ annually** added to property tax bill
- Adds **$200–$400/month** to mortgage payment
- Lenders treat these as part of total housing expense — **affects loan qualification**

**Legal Requirement:**
- Texas Property Code Section 5.014 requires disclosure before contract execution
- If notice isn't provided, buyer may terminate contract **even on closing day**
- **Many buyers report the disclosure was buried in paperwork they didn't fully read**

**Video Talking Point:** "Your builder shows you a monthly payment at the model home. That number doesn't include the MUD or PID assessment. You find out what your REAL payment is when you get your first property tax bill — and it's $200-400 more per month than you budgeted."

## THE PREFERRED LENDER RACKET

### How It Works
1. Builder offers $5K-$10K in "closing cost assistance" or "design center credits" IF you use their preferred lender
2. Preferred lender quotes rates **.25-.50% higher** than competitive market rates
3. Buyers think they're getting a deal — but the higher rate costs more over the loan life

### The Math (for the video)
- $300,000 home
- Builder lender: 6.75% with $7,000 closing credit
- Outside lender: 6.25% with $0 credit
- Monthly difference: ~$100/month
- 30-year cost of the "free" $7K credit: **~$36,000 in extra interest**

### Legal Reality
- RESPA prohibits kickbacks and referral fees — but the preferred lender structure is technically legal
- Builder **cannot refuse to sell** based on lender choice
- Builder CAN reduce or eliminate incentives if you don't use their lender

### What Taylor Should Tell Viewers
"Get pre-approved with 2-3 outside lenders FIRST. Then compare their rates to the builder's lender. Run the math on the incentive vs. the rate difference over 10, 15, and 30 years. In most cases, the 'free money' costs you more than double."

## THE UPGRADE MARKUP SCAM

### Base Price vs. Reality
- The model home you tour is loaded with **upgrades not included in base price**
- Buyers routinely end up **$50K–$100K+ over base price**

### Builder Upgrade Margins
- $3,000 granite countertop upgrade → **builder cost: ~$1,200** (150% markup)
- $8,000 hardwood flooring package → **builder cost: ~$3,500** (128% markup)
- Builders make significantly higher margins on upgrades than base construction
- This incentivizes keeping base prices artificially low while pushing expensive upgrades

### What's NOT Included (Surprises)
- **Lot premiums:** $10,000–$75,000+ depending on location/view
- **Landscaping:** Front/back yard often bare dirt at closing
- **Fencing:** Almost never included
- **Appliances:** Refrigerator frequently NOT included
- **Blinds/curtains:** Not included
- **Ceiling fans:** Often not included
- **Utility connections:** Extra fees if development is still under construction
- **Post-build essentials (fencing, landscaping, blinds, appliances):** Add **$25,000–$50,000**

### Video Talking Point
"The price on the sign outside the model home? That's the starting price for the smallest floor plan on the worst lot with zero upgrades. By the time you pick the lot you actually want and the finishes that match the model you walked through, you're $50K-$80K higher. And you still don't have a fence, blinds, or a refrigerator."

## TEXAS WARRANTY LAW — WHAT BUYERS NEED TO KNOW

### Standard Builder Warranty Structure (1-2-10)
- **Year 1:** Covers workmanship and materials defects
- **Year 2:** Covers electrical, plumbing, HVAC, and mechanical systems
- **Year 10:** Covers major structural defects only

### The Catch
- **Texas does NOT legally require builders to provide a warranty**
- Warranty agreements are written to favor the builder with complex exclusions
- Many items classified as "cosmetic" or "wear and tear" to deny claims
- Strict notice requirements — miss a deadline and lose your claim

### Dispute Process (RCLA)
- Written notice to builder: **60 days before** legal action
- Builder has **35 days** to inspect
- Builder has **45 days** to submit written settlement offer
- If accepted, builder has **45 days** to make repairs

### Video Talking Point
"Your builder's warranty isn't what you think it is. It's written by their lawyers, not consumer advocates. Year 1 covers cosmetic stuff — nail pops, paint. Year 2 covers systems — but they'll argue about what's 'normal settling' vs. a defect. Year 10 covers structural — but good luck proving a structural defect when the builder controls the inspection process."
