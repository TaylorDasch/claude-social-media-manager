**Ring logic — what feeds what:**

- **PILLAR pulls** broad relocation search ("new construction Temple TX," "best builders Temple," map intent). It's the URL Taylor sends to every cold lead. It links OUT to every other ring.
- **BUILDER DEEP-DIVES pull** branded search ("Omega Builders review," "Carothers homes Temple"). They link to matching warnings (e.g., Carothers deep-dive links to the Design Center Markup warning) and matching comparisons (Carothers vs Bella Terra resale).
- **WARNINGS pull** highest CTR (proven pattern), highest loss-aversion retention, and highest trust signal. They link back to the pillar + the comparison that solves the problem.
- **COMPARISONS pull** decision-stage viewers (60–120 days from move). They link to two builder deep-dives + the relevant warning.
- **SHORTS pull** discovery-stage viewers via TikTok/Reels/Shorts feed. Each Short carries one DM keyword and a Related Video link to its long-form parent.
- **PATTERN-BREAKS pull** brand-defining attention. They feed everything by anchoring Taylor's category position.

**The compounding loop:**
A viewer sees a Short → DMs the keyword → gets the lead magnet PDF → that PDF mentions 2 long-forms → those long-forms link to the pillar → the pillar links to the comparison most relevant to their situation → the comparison ends with a Calendly CTA. **One Short can pull a viewer through the entire ring in a single session.** Most agents post Shorts that lead to nothing.

**Working title:** "Warning: The Design Center Markup That Turns $5K of Upgrades Into $25K at the Builder"
- Char count: 86.

**Local moat test:** Universal pattern, but localized via specific Temple builder examples + named Temple-area contractors (granite, flooring, electrical) for post-closing alternatives. Walk past a real builder design center on B-roll. **PASS with localization.**

**Hook (3 options):**
- **A (chosen):** "Granite countertop upgrade at the builder's design center: $11,200. The exact same granite, installed by a Temple stone fabricator after closing: $4,800. That's a $6,400 markup on one upgrade. A typical Temple new construction buyer leaves fifteen-to-twenty-five thousand dollars on the table at the design center — and most of them don't realize it until 60 days after they've closed. Today I'm showing you exactly which upgrades to delete from your build and which Temple contractors to call after closing."
- B: "I sat with a buyer at a Temple builder's design center last month and watched them get quoted $48,000 in upgrades on a $310,000 base build. By the end of the appointment we cut $19,000 out of the order — same finished house, $19,000 lower mortgage. Today I'm showing you exactly how."
- C: "Every builder in Temple has a design center. Every design center has a markup. The markup runs 2x to 5x retail on the same upgrades. There are 3 upgrades worth taking from the builder. Everything else, you delete and source after closing. Here's the list."

**Value preview:** "We'll cover: actual price comparisons on 5 common upgrades (granite, flooring, lighting, paint, fence), the 3 upgrades that ARE worth taking from the builder, the mortgage math on a $25K design center bill at today's rates, and the 4 Temple contractors I'd call the day after closing."

**3 "so what" beats:**
1. The design center markup is fixed margin to the builder — it's not negotiation-flexible. The way to reduce it is to delete the upgrade from the build and source it post-closing. Even at full retail from a local Temple contractor, you typically come out 30–60% cheaper.
2. There are 3 upgrades that ARE worth taking from the builder because rebuilding them is structural: floor plan changes, electrical pre-wires, and anything inside the wall before drywall. Everything else (countertops, flooring, lighting, paint colors, fencing, landscaping) is post-closing fair game.
3. The cost of the upgrade isn't just the markup — it's the loan implications. A $25K upgrade wraps into the mortgage at the build price and adds roughly $165/month at 7% over 30 years.
