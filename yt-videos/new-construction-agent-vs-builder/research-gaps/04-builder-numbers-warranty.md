## 1. NAHB "What Home Buyers Really Want"

**Most recent editions located:**
- **2024 edition** — released April 2024; sample = "more than 3,000 recent and prospective home buyers."
- **2025 buyer preferences release** — February 2025 press release on affordability-driven shifts.

**Key findings (both editions combined):**
- Median home size buyers want: **2,067 sq ft (2024 ed.)** vs 2,260 sq ft in 2003 — a ~20-year downward shift.
- Average new-home size built: dropped from **2,479 sq ft (2022) → 2,411 sq ft (2023) → 2,150 sq ft median (2024)** — lowest in 15 years (Eye On Housing, NAHB).
- **38% of builders** built smaller homes in 2023 to support sales; **26%** planned to build even smaller in 2024.
- **64% of builders offering sales incentives**; **33% cutting home prices** (NAHB Feb 2025 release).
- 66% of buyers prefer single-family detached; 16% townhome; 10% multifamily/condo.
- Top upgrade willingness: 70%+ pay for gourmet kitchen; 65% for primary bath upgrade.
- Energy Star appliances + windows = top essential features.
- Technology features rising: security cameras, wired security, smart thermostats, video doorbells, multi-zone HVAC.

**Direct quote (citation-ready):**
> "People are being less influenced by influencers... Buyers are becoming more sophisticated in what they want." — Doug Cummins, DAHLIN Group (NAHB Feb 2025).

**Trend direction vs prior years:**
- Buyer-preferred size DOWN ~8.5% over 20 years.
- Built-home size DOWN 13.3% (2022 → 2024 median).
- Incentive prevalence UP sharply — **60% of builders offered special incentives June 2024 – Feb 2025** (AEI / NAHB data).

**GAP — not found in NAHB public materials:**
- NAHB "What Home Buyers Really Want" does NOT break out agent usage by new vs existing construction. That stat lives in the NAR Profile (63% new vs 92% resale) — already in core research.
- NAHB public press does not publish buyer satisfaction scores or top-regret percentages. Regret data comes from Clever Real Estate (82% of 2023/24 buyers have at least one regret; 28% cite "home requires too much maintenance" as top regret — buyer's remorse / Clever Real Estate 2024).
- Eye On Housing intro posts in the 2024 series are topical (size, design) — no agent-usage analysis in public-facing articles.

## 2. Concession Negotiation (Represented vs Unrepresented)

**What IS publicly documented:**
- **60% of builders offered special incentives** June 2024 – Feb 2025 (mortgage rate buydowns, closing-cost credits, flex dollars). Source: AEI / NAHB builder data.
- **Buydown cost to the builder (sized as % of home sale price):**
  - Full-term permanent buydown: up to **6%** of sale price
  - 3-2-1 temporary buydown: **~4%** of sale price
  - 2-1 temporary buydown: **~2%** of sale price
- In Feb 2025, majority of builders said they could buy down fixed rates **into the high 4%s to high 5%s** on a sale price.
- Conventional (Fannie/Freddie) seller-concession cap: **3–6%**; FHA cap: **6%**; when buyer puts <10% down on conventional, cap drops to **3%**.
- Builder incentives weighted heavily toward entry-level and first-time buyers; **~50% of one public builder's Q4 closings with forward-commitment rate locks were first-time buyers**.
- NAR 2024 Profile: **88% of all buyers** used an agent; **63% for newly built homes** vs **92% for previously owned**. No concession split by representation.
- Buyer-agent satisfaction: **88% of buyers would use their agent again or recommend**; **~90% satisfied** with responsiveness, market knowledge, honesty, people skills.

**GAP — no public dataset splits concessions by represented vs unrepresented:**
- NAR 2024 Profile does not segment concessions by buyer-agent representation.
- Zillow Research, HomeLight, Redfin, ATTOM, and Texas Realtors association data **do not break out** "represented vs walk-in" concession averages for new construction specifically.
- This is a genuine industry-data gap that Taylor can call out on camera: "There is no public dataset that compares what represented buyers get vs walk-ins in new construction — but builders are giving up 2–6% of sale price in incentives, and walk-ins don't know the ceiling."

**Usable on-camera line:**
> "Builders offered incentives on 60% of new-home sales from mid-2024 to early 2025 — some worth up to 6% of the purchase price. NAR doesn't publish how much of that represented buyers captured vs walk-ins. That's not an accident — it's the gap an agent closes."

## 3. Third-Party Warranty Companies

### 2-10 Home Buyers Warranty (2-10 HBW)
- **BBB Rating: A+** (BBB-accredited since March 2026 on the Memphis TN profile)
- **Total complaints last 3 years: 665**
- **Complaints closed last 12 months: 223**
- **Complaint mix:** Service/Repair = **558**; Sales/Advertising = **37**; Product = **24**; Order = **22**; Customer Service = **10**
- **Resolution signal:** Of 665 complaints, **544 "Answered" but not accepted by the consumer; only 121 marked "Resolved."** Translation: the business replies, but consumers aren't satisfied with the resolution ~82% of the time.
- **Common denial patterns (from complaint narratives):**
  - Claims denied when items "remain under manufacturer warranty"
  - Reimbursement limited to network contractor rates when consumer uses non-network services
  - Authorization/pre-approval disputes (consumers claim insufficient notice of pre-approval requirements)
  - Extended claims-processing delays
- **Consumer Affairs / Trustpilot:** Below-average scores on both; repeated mentions of denied claims, delays, poor customer service.
- **Denial rate percentage:** GAP — no company-published or regulator-published denial rate exists for 2-10. The 82% "answered-but-not-resolved" ratio above is the closest proxy.
- **Source:** https://www.bbb.org/us/tn/memphis/profile/home-warranty-plans/2-10-home-buyers-warranty-0543-44188479/complaints | accessed 2026-04-17

### StrucSure Home Warranty
- **BBB Rating: A-**
- **Accreditation: NOT BBB-accredited**
- **Complaint volume:** Low on the Denver BBB page (1 unresolved complaint referenced) — but complaint pattern across consumer review sites is meaningful.
- **Business model red flag:** StrucSure itself states in response to complaints that it acts as **administrator/call center** and forwards service requests to the **builder** for coverage determination. Translation: the warranty company offloads coverage decisions back to the builder.
- **Common denial themes:** doors, wall cracks, windows denied as "homeowner responsibility"; faulty siding/window installation labeled out-of-scope; slow response; poor email/phone communication; limited coverage compared to marketing.
- **Consumer characterization (direct quote):** *"This is literally just a cushion for builder."*
- **Source:** https://www.bbb.org/us/co/denver/profile/home-warranty-plans/strucsure-home-warranty-1296-9031173 | accessed 2026-04-17

### Blackland Prairie / Expansive Soil Maintenance Clause

**Closest verbatim / paraphrased warranty language found:**

From the **Texas Residential Construction Commission (TRCC) Limited Statutory Warranty and Building & Performance Standards** (the statutory framework that Texas builder warranties typically mirror):

> "The homeowner is responsible for reasonable maintenance of uniform soil moisture content around exterior flatwork."

> "Alterations to, or the failure to maintain, the grade of the soil that are not in compliance with the Code, applicable governmental regulations or in compliance with an applicable drainage plan [are excluded from builder responsibility]."

> "Homeowners shall not over-water surrounding soil or allow the surrounding soil to become excessively dry."

> "Changes in the underground water table that exerts pressure on, seeps, or leaks under the home... or causes subsidence or sinkholes [are excluded]."

> "Damage caused by tree roots absorbing soil moisture near the foundation [is excluded]."

> "Erosion or accretion of soils (unless such loss or damage is a direct result of a construction defect) [is excluded]."

Additional consumer-facing builder language from North Texas builder-warranty guidance:
> "Damage from lack of foundation watering" — explicitly non-covered.
> "Soil movement from poor drainage" — not covered.
> "Ignoring small issues often voids coverage."

**Note on 2-10 HBW Texas-specific position:**
- 2-10's Texas page affirmatively states: *"2-10's warranty does not exclude 'drought.' After all, some parts of Texas are always experiencing drought."* — a marketing counter-message, notable because it's responding to a known industry friction point. It does NOT remove the broader soil-maintenance exclusions; it only says drought itself isn't a named exclusion.
- Source: https://www.2-10.com/builders-warranty/texas-home-builder-warranty/ | accessed 2026-04-17

**Blackland Prairie / Bell County relevance:**
- Blackland Prairie belt runs through east-central Texas including parts of Bell County (Temple is on the western edge of transition into Grand Prairie / Edwards Plateau soils, but expansive-clay issues appear throughout the metroplex and I-35 corridor).
- Expansive clay can expand up to **30%+ in volume** when absorbing water — generates major uplift pressure on slabs.
- The shrink-swell cycle (summer contraction, spring expansion) is cited as the **#1 cause of foundation movement in DFW** — and the maintenance-clause framework puts responsibility for managing that on the homeowner, not the builder.
