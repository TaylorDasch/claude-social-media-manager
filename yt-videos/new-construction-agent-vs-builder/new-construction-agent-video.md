As of the most recent Texas-specific buyer survey window (transactions July 2023–June 2024), **30% of Texas buyers purchased new construction**—about **2× the national share** reported in that same Texas survey release. citeturn7search0turn7search3 Nationally, the latest NAR buyer survey highlights show **63% of “new home” buyers used a real estate agent**, versus **92%** of buyers of previously owned homes—meaning **new construction is the category where buyers most frequently go unrepresented**. citeturn7search30

## How much new-construction pricing and sales data never hits MLS in Texas

**MLS is not a “public registry,” and there is no Texas law that forces a builder to report a sale to MLS.** MLS participation is a membership/contractual system; its “reporting rules” bind MLS participants, not builders as a class. The practical consequence is that MLS-based market stats can be incomplete for new builds—especially when builders sell directly through on-site sales staff. citeturn3search6turn5search4

A key Texas-specific data point is buried in how major Texas housing datasets are assembled: entity["organization","Texas Real Estate Research Center","Texas A&M University"] explicitly notes that its MLS-based housing activity data **does not include new home sales negotiated directly between builder and buyer outside MLS**. citeturn3search0 That is an unusually clear, Texas-relevant acknowledgment that “MLS = not the whole market” for new builds.

**What you likely want for the video (but public data doesn’t cleanly provide):**
- *“How often do Lennar / D.R. Horton / Centex list and close through MLS vs internal?”*  
- *“What % of new construction sales are unreported to MLS (US and Texas)?”*  

After extensive searching, I did **not** find a credible (methodologically transparent) public estimate that reduces this to a single national or Texas percentage. The closest high-confidence statement available from authoritative Texas sources is the structural one: **MLS-based datasets omit builder-direct transactions.** citeturn3search0  
**Content opportunity:** Call out the *absence* of transparent statewide reporting and explain why buyers should treat “MLS comps” as a potentially incomplete sample set for certain builder communities.

**Texas non-disclosure amplifies the problem.** In Texas, sales price data is not consistently available from public record filings the way it is in disclosure states, which increases reliance on MLS and other private datasets for closed-price comp work. A Texas appraiser disciplinary document captures this reality plainly: the appraiser emphasized Texas is a non-disclosure state and that if a sale isn’t in MLS, verification becomes materially harder. citeturn8search27 A national explainer on non-disclosure states also notes that limited access to sold data increases dependence on MLS access and professional networks for pricing intel. citeturn8search31

**Bottom line (data-backed):** In Texas, when a builder sale doesn’t get entered into MLS, you often lose the easiest “single source of truth” for closed-price verification—because public records are not as complete on price as in disclosure states. citeturn3search0turn8search27turn8search31

## How builder “preferred lenders” create incentive pressure and appraisal risk

Many large builders operate (or are affiliated with) mortgage and settlement-service companies and commonly tie buyer incentives to using those affiliates. A concrete example: a Lennar promotion’s fine print states that **interest rate and closing cost offers require financing through Lennar Mortgage**. citeturn4search8

### What regulators actually require buyers to receive: AfBA disclosures
When a real estate provider refers a consumer to an affiliated settlement-service provider, U.S. federal law (RESPA) governs “Affiliated Business Arrangements” (AfBAs). The CFPB explains AfBAs, including that the referring party has an ownership/beneficial interest relationship with the provider being referred and that disclosure requirements apply. citeturn4search2

### Incentives vs. “requirement” (how builders thread the needle)
In practice, many builders do not “require” the in-house lender outright—but **the incentive structure can make the choice economically coercive** (e.g., rate buydowns, lender credits, or closing-cost contributions only if the buyer uses the builder’s affiliate). The publicly documented Lennar example above is a clean, citable illustration of how this is presented to consumers. citeturn4search8

### Appraisal conflict: what’s proven vs. what’s “risk logic”
**Data-backed:** New-construction pricing today is heavily incentive-driven in many markets (rate buydowns, discounts, etc.). Major housing research reporting in late 2025 shows builders expanded discounting and rate incentives, which adds complexity to “true net price” comparisons. citeturn8search2turn8search6

**Risk logic (flagged as inference):** When lender incentives are tied to a builder affiliate, the affiliate lender is economically motivated to help deals close (because closings drive builder absorption and lender profit). That creates an obvious *conflict-of-incentives* perception for consumers, even when formal appraisal-independence rules exist. I did **not** find a high-quality study that cleanly compares “builder-affiliated lender appraisals vs independent lender appraisals” specifically for new construction in Texas markets (this is a real gap in public research).

### Enforcement / lawsuits (what I could document)
I did find litigation reporting involving a production builder and its preferred lender alleging deceptive mortgage-payment advertising practices (reported as a class action). citeturn4search3 This is not an appraisal-coercion case—but it does support the broader point that builder + affiliated/preferred financing structures have generated consumer litigation risk.

## What’s inside production-builder contracts that can trap unrepresented buyers in Texas

### Builders often use builder-drafted contracts, not the “standard resale form”
Texas real estate license holders commonly use promulgated forms in many transactions, but builders (especially production builders selling directly) frequently rely on **builder proprietary contracts** drafted for their business model (extended build timelines, change-order regimes, limitation-of-liability structures, arbitration, etc.). The consumer-facing implication is straightforward: **you’re usually negotiating on the builder’s paper**, not a neutral template. This “builder contract is builder-favorable” framing is consistently echoed in consumer/legal explainers focused on new construction. citeturn40search1turn40search0

### Clauses that commonly disadvantage buyers (with Texas relevance)
The following themes are repeatedly cited in new-construction contract explainers and consumer guidance (note: prevalence varies by builder and community; buyers need contract-specific review):
- **Non-refundable deposits + milestone-based forfeiture risk** (often larger than resale earnest money; can become non-refundable after certain milestones). citeturn40search1turn40search0  
- **Completion date flexibility / delay risk** (buyers are often advised to “pad the timeline” and expect delays, which can be financially painful when leases or rate locks expire). citeturn40search2  
- **Change-order structures** where modifications trigger admin fees/markups and strict deadlines. citeturn40search1  
- **Warranty limitations + narrow definitions** of what qualifies for “structural” coverage. citeturn40search1turn40search0  
- **Mandatory arbitration** clauses or warranty-program dispute procedures that limit courtroom remedies. citeturn40search1  

### Texas construction-defect dispute pathway: Chapter 27 (RCLA)
Texas has a formal pre-suit notice and opportunity-to-inspect/cure framework for construction defects under **Texas Property Code Chapter 27** (Residential Construction Liability Act). Secondary legal summaries quote the statutory requirement that homeowners provide notice before filing suit or initiating arbitration, and that the notice must describe the defect and reference Chapter 27. citeturn6search37turn6search29  
**Practical impact:** even if a buyer believes they have a clear defect claim, Texas’s process can create delay and procedural traps—another reason contract review and documentation discipline matter.

### Disclosure: what Texas buyers may *not* get on new builds
Texas seller disclosure obligations (commonly cited under Property Code §5.008) are widely discussed for resale, but common summaries note exemptions in certain circumstances—often including properties never occupied. citeturn6search36turn6search32  
**Content opportunity:** “You might not get the same seller disclosure package on a brand-new, never-occupied home—so your due diligence has to shift to inspections, specifications, and contract language.”

## What buyer agents actually change in new construction—with real stats

### Representation rates: new construction is where buyers most often go without an agent
NAR’s survey highlights show:
- **88%** of all home buyers purchased through an agent/broker.
- When split by home type: **63% of new home buyers used an agent**, compared to **92%** for previously owned homes. citeturn7search30turn7search7  
This means the “I don’t need an agent for a builder purchase” idea shows up statistically in a way it doesn’t for resale.

### Texas new construction share: it’s not a niche segment here
Texas buyer research reports **30%** of Texas buyers purchased new construction in the survey window (July 2023–June 2024). citeturn7search0turn7search3  
That makes “new construction without representation” a *large-scale* consumer-risk problem in Texas—not an edge case.

### Post–NAR settlement mechanics: what changed after August 17, 2024
NAR’s settlement FAQ and policy summaries explain the MLS policy changes implemented in 2024, including the removal of offers of compensation from MLS displays and the increased importance of written buyer agreements in MLS-participant workflows. citeturn7search2turn7search24  
A downstream, buyer-facing reality: **compensation can still be paid by the seller/builder**, but it’s more explicitly a negotiated term rather than an MLS-advertised field. citeturn7search24turn7search23

### Are builders still offering co-op compensation?
Industry reporting in late 2024 indicated many builders **had not** broadly cut buyer-agent compensation yet, while they monitored post-settlement changes. citeturn43search16turn7search9 Some builders publicly communicated continued agent-compensation programs post-settlement (example: a national builder’s agent portal message referencing the August 17, 2024 settlement and ongoing compensation programs). citeturn7search17  
**Important caveat:** terms can vary by community, inventory status, and time; buyers should confirm in writing before first registration/model-home visit (a recurring theme in post-settlement explainers). citeturn40search1turn7search24

### What agents negotiate that unrepresented buyers often miss (mixed evidence)
**Documented as common negotiation “pressure points”:**
- Builders may not move much on base price, but they often compete via **incentives** like rate buydowns and other concessions; buyers are explicitly advised to focus on incentives and the “right things” to negotiate. citeturn40search2turn46search1  
- Buyers are repeatedly advised to plan for timelines/delays and protect themselves on moving logistics. citeturn40search2  

**Anecdotal / practitioner-based (flagged):** lot selection impacts (future resale, traffic, drainage), structural upgrade prioritization, change-order discipline, and construction-stage oversight are areas where experienced buyer agents add value—but these are not well quantified in public datasets.

## Why overpaying for new construction can backfire on resale in Texas

### The “new home premium” is real—but it moves (and can evaporate)
A Texas-based housing research analysis found the national new-home premium has narrowed dramatically over time—down to about **1.5% by 1Q2025** (example medians cited: ~$417k new vs ~$410k existing). citeturn8search4  
Separately, price-per-square-foot analysis showed that by mid-2024 **new homes were selling at a per-square-foot discount vs existing** in the Zillow dataset, reflecting heavy builder competition and incentive use in some markets. citeturn8search21turn8search5

**What that means for resale risk (data-backed + inference):**
- If the “new premium” is small (or negative), a buyer who overpays relative to the true local resale comp set has less cushion to exit in years 1–3.
- Incentives (rate buydowns, closing credits) can make “net effective price” lower than headline price; if your resale buyer can buy the next phase with incentives, your resale competes against subsidized pricing. Widespread discounting on new builds was observed in late 2025 analyses. citeturn8search2turn8search6  

### Texas non-disclosure compounds comp and appraisal friction
In non-disclosure states, limited public sale-price visibility increases reliance on MLS/private datasets, and missing sales can make comp discovery harder. citeturn8search31turn8search27  
That matters if you need to justify a resale price (or if an appraiser needs verifiable closed sales). A Texas appraiser disciplinary document explicitly describes dependence on MLS for sales history in a non-disclosure context. citeturn8search27

### Builders’ phase pricing strategy can whipsaw perceived values
Public market reporting shows builders have materially increased discounts and incentives in recent periods. citeturn8search2turn8search6  
**Inference (flagged):** when later phases are released with different incentives, a buyer who paid a top-of-phase price (especially with large option packages) can see resale competition from “brand new with incentives” homes down the street—reducing resale liquidity even if the metro median is flat-to-up.

## The Texas legal and regulatory backdrop buyers need to understand

### Texas agency structure: “intermediary,” not traditional dual agency
Texas allows a broker to facilitate a transaction as an **intermediary** only with required consent and statutory guardrails. TREC explains intermediary relationships and cites the relevant statutory sections (TRELA). citeturn9search0turn9search5  
TREC also explains the **Information About Brokerage Services (IABS)** form and how it outlines representation/non-representation status—important context when buyers walk into a builder sales office assuming the on-site rep “works for them.” citeturn9search1turn9search21

### New construction defects: Chapter 27 notice + opportunity-to-cure process
Texas Property Code Chapter 27 (Residential Construction Liability Act) requires pre-suit notice and opportunity to inspect/cure before litigation/arbitration for construction defects, as summarized in legal references quoting the statute. citeturn6search37turn6search29

### Texas implied warranties in builder-vendor sales
Texas Supreme Court case law (Humber v. Morton) is a foundational decision recognizing implied warranties in certain new-home builder-vendor sales contexts (consumer-protection shift away from strict caveat emptor for new homes). citeturn9search2  
**Practical reality:** builder contracts and written warranties still matter because dispute processes and remedies can be shaped by contract terms and statutory procedures. citeturn40search1turn6search37

### Time limits on construction claims (not Property Code, but critical)
Texas maintains statutes of repose/limitations for construction-related claims in the Civil Practice & Remedies Code; the official Texas statutes site is the most stable reference point for the text. citeturn9search7  
**Why it matters for buyers:** you can’t treat “10-year structural warranty” marketing as the same thing as unlimited legal recourse.

### Pending Texas legislation (as of April 10, 2026)
I did not identify a clearly relevant, currently pending Texas legislative change (tracked in public sources retrieved here) specifically aimed at “new construction transparency” or “mandatory buyer representation.” However, Texas real estate law does update—e.g., Texas assignment disclosure requirements were summarized in a Texas housing research explainer (effective-date details depend on the statute and transaction type). citeturn6search27  
**Content opportunity:** “The law changes; builders don’t slow down to explain it—your representation is the early-warning system.”
