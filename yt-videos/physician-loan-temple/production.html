How It Works — The 5 Benefits

1:30 – 4:30
Setup D: Screen share + facecam corner
"Here's how a physician mortgage loan works. There are five key benefits that make it different from a conventional loan."
"Number one — zero or low down payment. Most lenders offer 0% down up to $750K to a million dollars. 5% down gets you up to $1.5 million. Compare that to a conventional loan where you need 5-20% down on anything.

Number two — no PMI. Ever. This is the big one. On a conventional loan with less than 20% down, you pay private mortgage insurance. On a $300K loan, that's $125 to $375 a month, just gone. Physician loans waive PMI completely. That saves you $1,500 to $4,500 a year.

Number three — student loan treatment. This is the game-changer. A conventional lender looks at your $250K in student debt and calculates 1% of the balance as your monthly obligation. That's $2,500 a month in debt that doesn't actually exist as a payment. You'd get denied instantly. A physician lender uses your actual IBR payment — which during residency is often zero to $300 a month. That single difference can increase your purchasing power by $100K to $200K.

Number four — employment contracts accepted. You don't need two years of W-2 history. A signed employment contract or match letter is enough. A PGY-1 matched to BSW in March can close on a Temple home in May — before their first paycheck.

Number five — high loan limits. Conforming loans cap at $806,500 in 2026. Physician loans go to a million, $1.5 million, even $2.5 million depending on the lender. In Temple, you'll never need that much — but it's there."
✎ On-screen: Number each benefit with a graphic card (1-5). Show the student loan math side-by-side: "Conventional sees: $2,500/mo" vs "Physician lender sees: $250/mo" with visual comparison.

The Catch — What They Don't Tell You

4:30 – 7:00
Setup A: Talking Head — direct, trust-building
"Okay, now here's the part every lender video skips. The catches.

Catch number one: you're paying a rate premium. Physician loans run about a quarter to a half percent higher than what you'd get on a conventional loan with 20% down. On a $300K loan, that's $50 to $100 more per month — or $18,000 to $36,000 over 30 years. If you have the cash for 20% down, conventional is almost always cheaper.

Catch number two: the ARM trap. A lot of physician loans are offered as adjustable rate mortgages because the initial rate looks amazing — like 5.75%. But after 5 years, it adjusts. If rates haven't come down, your $2,000 payment could become $2,600 overnight. If you're a resident buying for 3-5 years and you KNOW you're leaving, an ARM can save you $12K to $24K. If you're settling in — always go 30-year fixed."
