## 3:00 — MISTAKE TWO: IGNORING THE FEMA FLOOD MAP (2 min 30 sec)

**[VISUAL: Taylor pointing at FEMA flood map on a tablet or laptop screen]**

>
> **Ignoring the FEMA flood map.**
>
> Here's the scenario I watched play out.
>
> Buyer found a 2,100 square foot home in North Temple near a creek corridor. **Asking price $319K.** Appraised at $325K.
> `[1]` Perfect deal on paper.
>
> **They skipped the flood map check.**
>
> At closing, the lender required **flood insurance** because the property sits in a **FEMA Zone AE.**
> The quote came in at **$2,400 a year.**
>
> That's **$200 a month** added to their PITI payment.
> Over a 30-year loan, that's **$72,000.**
>
> Not counting the fact that they **could not resell at the same pace** because every future buyer runs the same math.

**[VISUAL: FEMA flood map zoomed into Temple, Bell County]**

> Here's what to do — and it takes **60 seconds:**
>
> Go to **msc.fema.gov.**
> Type the address.
> Click "search."
>
> You'll see one of three colors:
> - **Zone X** — minimal risk. **Go.**
> - **Zone AE, A, AH, AO** — these are the ones that trigger flood insurance. **Pause. Run the math.**
> - **Zone V** — rare in Temple but high risk. **Do not buy.**

**[VISUAL: Taylor back on camera]**

> **Specifically in Temple**, the risk zones cluster near:
> - **Pepper Creek** on the north side
> - **Little River** tributaries in southwest Temple
> - Low-lying parcels in **older South Temple** near drainage
>
> If your property touches any of those — **check the map.** Don't trust the listing.
> Listings **don't disclose zone by default.** You have to ask for the elevation certificate or check FEMA yourself.

## 5:30 — MISTAKE THREE: TAKING THE BUILDER'S LENDER WITHOUT RUNNING THE MATH (3 min)

**[VISUAL: Taylor at a builder's model home sign or in front of a new construction home]**

> **Mistake number three.**
>
> `[1]` This one is **specific to new construction in Temple — and Temple has a lot of new construction.**
>
> **Taking the builder's preferred lender without running the math.**
>
> Here's how it works.
>
> Builder offers you **"$10,000 in closing cost incentives"** — if you use their preferred lender.
> That number sounds great.
> Most buyers stop thinking and say yes.
>
> **Here's what they don't tell you.**

**[VISUAL: Simple graphic or Taylor writing on a whiteboard showing the math]**

> The builder's preferred lender often has a **higher rate** than the market.
>
> I watched a buyer last spring take a builder's $10K incentive and a **7.125% rate.**
> Market rate at the time on a **conventional**, same profile: **6.625%.**
>
> On a $380,000 loan, **half a percent higher** over 30 years is:
> **Twenty dollars more per month** interest.
> **Seven thousand two hundred dollars more over 30 years** — just in interest.
>
> `[1]` But here's the kicker — most buyers refinance in 5-7 years anyway. So the real cost is more like **$6,800 over the first 5 years alone.**
>
> That $10K incentive?
> **Shrinks to $3,200 net.**
> After fees, it could be **net negative.**

**[VISUAL: Taylor back on camera · direct]**

> The fix — and this is the one **nobody tells you:**
>
> **You can always bring your own lender.**
> Builders **cannot force you** to use their lender.
> Some builders will **still give you partial closing-cost credit** even if you bring your own — especially if you ask directly in the purchase agreement.
>
> The move:
> **One.** Get pre-approved with a competitive lender BEFORE you walk into the model home.
> Veterans United if you're VA. Navy Fed for anyone. A local credit union. Your broker.
> **Two.** Get the builder's lender quote.
> **Three.** Run both full loan estimates side-by-side for 30 years.
> **Four.** Take the one where the **all-in cost is lower** — not the one with the biggest closing-cost credit.
>
> Nine times out of ten, your outside lender wins by $3,000 to $8,000 over the life of the loan.
> Even after the builder incentive.

**Energy:** Authoritative. You're warning people. This is not a salesy video — it's a consumer-protection vibe.

**Tone by section:**
- Hook: serious. "$18,000" and "three days before closing" — say those with weight.
