## MARKET SUMMARY

The Temple market in early 2026 is a buyer's market with nuance. Prices are running 2–7% below year-ago levels, days on market are stretching longer across every price band, and more than half of all active listings have already taken a price cut. Volume has nearly stabilized — March 2026 matched March 2025 almost exactly — which suggests the market is finding a floor, not falling off one. The clearest opportunities are in the $200K–$300K resale segment (the engine of this market) and in select new-construction subdivisions where builders are motivated. The clearest danger zone is the $400K–$500K band, where 19.6 months of inventory means sellers are in a genuinely difficult position.

### Narrative

The $200K–$300K band is the beating heart of this market — 57 of 105 March closings (54%) happened here. It is also where the most resale inventory sits (397 of 802 active listings), meaning buyers have options and sellers face real competition.

**Under $200K** is the relative bright spot. Volume jumped 40% month-over-month, median DOM is the lowest of any band at 93 days, and at 6.7 months of inventory it is the closest thing to a balanced market in Temple right now. These are often older homes or distressed properties, but they move. First-time buyers and cash investors compete here.

**$300K–$400K** is a clear buyer's market: 127 avg DOM and 11 months of supply. Buyers in this range should be negotiating confidently.

**$400K–$500K** is the alarm bell of this report. Five closings in all of March. Nineteen-point-six months of inventory. Average DOM of 154 days. Sellers here are not just competing with each other — they are competing with builders in the $300Ks who are actively cutting prices. This band demands either significant price reductions or exceptional product differentiation to sell.

New construction's share of closed sales grew from 5.4% to 22.9% in twelve months — a **4x increase**. This isn't just more homes being built; it reflects a significant shift in where buyers are looking and where builders are willing to deal.

### Builder Discount Reality

Builders are not holding firm on price. The data is clear:
- **Avg new construction price reduction: $21,995** from original list to close (vs. $16,149 for resale)
- **Mesa Ridge** (2025-built new construction) had 6 recent closings with avg orig-to-close ratios in the 87–93% range — meaning buyers are capturing **$15,000–$55,000 off original list prices**
- **Pecan Creek** is the exception: it moves fastest (avg 25 DOM on active listings) and sellers are holding closer to list

### What This Means for Your Audience

The standard assumption that "new construction is full price" does not hold in Temple's current market. A motivated buyer willing to shop slower-moving subdivisions (Hills of Westwood, Parks at Westfield, Grove at Lakewood Ranch) can negotiate builder-level discounts on brand-new product. The tradeoff is patience and understanding why those specific homes are slow to move.

### Market Characterization: Decelerating but Stabilizing

The headline: **volume has stopped falling, but prices haven't.** March 2026 matched March 2025 on transactions (+1.9%) while running nearly $5,000 lower on median price (-1.9%). The January YoY gap was -7.1% on price; by March it had narrowed to -1.9%. That's a market absorbing its correction, not accelerating lower.

Three consistent trends across Q1 2026:
1. **DOM is longer.** Every 2026 month runs 5–30 days longer than the same month in 2025. The market moves slowly now.
2. **Buyers are capturing more discount.** The avg orig-to-close ratio has declined from ~95–96% in early 2025 to ~92–93% in early 2026. That shift is real money.
3. **Prices per square foot are near 12-month lows**, despite a partial April bump — the trend is flat-to-declining, not recovering.

For a relocating buyer watching your channel: this data argues for acting before the stabilization turns into recovery. Volume is returning. Discounts are still available. The window isn't closed, but it's narrowing compared to a year ago.

### Data Transparency Note

MLS data anomaly: In this export, ClosePrice and CurrentPrice appear to be set equal at the time of closing for new construction transactions, which causes the current-list-to-close ratio to show as 100% for many new construction sales. **All ratio and discount analysis in this report uses OriginalListPrice vs. ClosePrice**, which is the accurate measure of true negotiation. Nine records from 2025 with clear data entry errors (e.g., $296 original list price) were excluded from ratio calculations.
