After 100+ deals in Temple, I can tell you — those calculators are missing at least three things that will wreck your returns. ### [0:30–1:30] THE PROPERTY

"Alright, here's the property. [PULL UP LISTING ON SCREEN] This is a [3-bed/2-bath, ~1,400 sqft] in [neighborhood], Temple TX. Listed at $200,000. Built in [year]. It's a pretty standard Temple rental candidate — brick, decent condition, no major red flags from the photos. Most investors would look at this and go 'what's the rent?' and that's about as deep as they get. We're going deeper."

### [1:30–2:30] STEP 1: PURCHASE MATH

"Step one — purchase math. I'm assuming 25% down on a conventional investment loan at 7% — that's what you're getting today unless you have a banking relationship.

- Purchase price: $200,000
- Down payment: $50,000
- Closing costs at 3%: $6,000
- Total cash in: $56,000
- Loan amount: $150,000
- Monthly P&I at 7%: $998

That's just principal and interest. That is NOT your total payment. This is where most calculators stop — and this is where most investors get burned."

### [2:30–4:30] STEP 2: THE REAL EXPENSES (What Calculators Miss)

"Step two — the real monthly expenses. And I'm going to show you the three things most people get wrong.

**Number one: property taxes.** Bell County's effective tax rate is 2.4 to 2.7%. On a $200K property, you're looking at $4,800 to $5,400 a YEAR. That's $400 to $450 a month. Most online calculators default to 1% or 1.2%. That's what you'd pay in most states. Texas is double that. If you use the wrong tax rate, you're off by $200 a month on day one.

**Number two: insurance.** Post-storm, post-Uri, insurance in Central Texas is running $2,200 to $2,800 a year for a rental property. NOT $1,200 like your calculator assumes. That's $185 to $235 a month.

**Number three: actual vacancy.** Everyone plugs in 5%. In Temple, the real number is closer to 8% when you factor in tenant turnover, make-ready time between tenants, and the fact that January and February are dead months for leasing. On a $1,650 rent, that's $132 a month, not $82.

So let's stack it up:
- P&I: $998
- Taxes: $425 (mid-range)
- Insurance: $210
- Vacancy reserve (8%): $132
- Maintenance reserve (5%): $82
- Property management (8%): $132
- CapEx reserve (5%): $82

**Total real monthly cost: $2,061**"

### [4:30–5:30] STEP 3: THE RENT

"Step three — what does this property actually rent for? Not Zillow's estimate. Not Rentometer. Actual comps.

I'm pulling recent leases in [neighborhood] from the MLS. Three-bed two-bath brick homes, 1,200 to 1,500 sqft:
- [Address 1]: $1,600/mo
- [Address 2]: $1,650/mo
- [Address 3]: $1,700/mo

Conservative rent estimate: $1,650 a month. I always underwrite to the middle, not the top."

### [5:30–6:30] STEP 4: THE VERDICT

"So here's the math:
- Monthly rent: $1,650
- Monthly expenses: $2,061
- **Monthly cash flow: negative $411**
- Cash-on-cash return on your $56K: **negative 8.8%**

At retail price and today's rates, this deal does not work as a conventional buy-and-hold. Period. And that's okay — that's the whole point of analyzing it properly. You just saved yourself from buying a deal that bleeds $400 a month.

But here's where it gets interesting..."

### [6:30–8:00] STEP 5: WHAT MAKES IT WORK

"There are three scenarios where this same property DOES work:

**Scenario A: You negotiate the price down to $165K.** Your P&I drops to $824. Total expenses drop to about $1,760. Now you're cash flowing $110 a month. Not amazing, but positive — and you have $35K in built equity day one.

**Scenario B: Rates drop to 5.5%.** Your P&I goes from $998 to $852. Total expenses: $1,915. You're close to break-even, and you've locked in a property that appreciates and builds equity.

**Scenario C: You run it as a mid-term rental.** Furnished, marketed to BSW traveling nurses. Instead of $1,650, you're getting $2,200 to $2,400 a month. Now this deal cash flows $200 to $400 positive even at retail price.

The deal doesn't change. YOUR STRATEGY changes. That's the difference between an investor who knows what they're doing and one who just bought a calculator off the internet."

### [8:00–8:45] THE FRAMEWORK RECAP

"So to recap — the five steps in under 5 minutes:

1. Purchase math — total cash in, not just down payment
2. Real expenses — Texas taxes, real insurance, actual vacancy
3. Actual rent comps — from the MLS, not Zillow
4. The verdict — honest cash flow number
5. What makes it work — negotiate, strategy shift, or wait

If you run every deal through this, you will never overpay for a rental property in Temple."
