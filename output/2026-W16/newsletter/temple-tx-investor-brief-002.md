## THE DEEP DIVE: Buying From Flippers — What They Fix, What They Skip, and How to Negotiate

Roughly 1 in 5 listings under $200K in Temple right now is LLC-owned. That means a flipper bought it, rehabbed it, and is trying to sell it for a margin. Some of these are excellent buys. Some are lipstick on a foundation crack. Here's how to tell the difference.

**What flippers almost always fix (because it photographs well):**
- Kitchen cabinets and countertops
- Bathroom tile and fixtures
- Flooring (usually LVP — cheap, durable, photographs like hardwood)
- Paint inside and out
- Light fixtures and ceiling fans
- Landscaping and curb appeal

This is the stuff that shows up in MLS photos and open houses. It's what moves the needle from $80K as-is to $145K "fully remodeled."

**What flippers frequently skip (because it's invisible and expensive):**
- Foundation repair — if the house "needs piers," that's $8-15K the flipper would rather not spend. Some do it. Many level the floors cosmetically and move on.
- Plumbing under the house — galvanized or cast iron pipes in a 1950s home are a ticking clock. Re-piping is $4-8K and nobody sees it in the listing photos.
- Electrical panel upgrades — a 100-amp panel on a home with new HVAC drawing 40+ amps is a fire risk, not a feature. Upgrading to 200-amp is $2-4K.
- Roof decking — new shingles over rotting decking is a 5-year problem. The roof "looks" new.
- Insulation — especially in pier and beam crawlspaces. Out of sight, out of mind.
- Sewer line — the line from the house to the street is the owner's responsibility. Camera scope it. A collapsed sewer line is a $5-10K surprise.

**How to evaluate a flip:**

Three questions that filter 90% of the risk:

1. **Pull the permit history.** If the flipper pulled permits for electrical, plumbing, and HVAC, they did it right. If they only pulled a "remodel" permit or nothing at all, the mechanical work may not be to code. Temple's permit records are public.

2. **Check the foundation independently.** Don't rely on the listing agent saying "foundation is fine." Spend $350-500 on a structural engineer's report. On a pier and beam home, get under the house yourself if you can. Look at the piers, joists, and subfloor.

3. **Compare the all-in cost.** If a flipper bought for $80K and is selling for $145K, that's a $65K spread. Rehab probably cost $30-40K. Their margin target is $20-30K. If you can verify the rehab quality is solid, you're still buying below replacement cost. A $145K turnkey property that would cost you $120K+ to replicate from scratch (purchase + rehab + holding costs + your time) is still a buy — you're paying a premium for convenience, not for overpricing.

**How to negotiate with a flipper:**

Flippers are not emotional sellers. They don't care about the house. They care about IRR — internal rate of return. Every month that property sits vacant is eating their margin through holding costs (mortgage, insurance, taxes, utilities). A flipper with a property that's been listed 30+ days is doing mental math on how much margin they're losing per week.

Use that. Your strongest negotiating tools:
- **Fast close.** Flippers want speed. Offer 14-21 day close with proof of funds or pre-approval and you'll get a better price than a higher offer with a 45-day close.
- **As-is with inspection contingency.** Tell them you'll buy as-is but you want a 7-day inspection period. Most flippers will take this because it signals you're serious but protects you.
- **Price reduction data.** If they've already cut the price once, they'll cut it again. Reference their original list price, the reduction, and current DOM. "You listed at $159K, cut to $145K after 30 days. You're now at 45 DOM. I'll close in 14 days at $133K." That's a conversation most flippers will have.

The deal in this week's autopsy — 1807 S 11th — is a textbook example. LLC-owned, price reduced $14,900, sitting vacant, concessions offered. The flipper did legitimate work (new HVAC, water heater, windows, kitchen). But the inspection is still non-negotiable. Especially on a 1952 pier and beam.

### THE GOOD

- **Sub-1-mile to three hospitals.** BSW (8,884 employees), VA Hospital, Central TX Veterans. Traveling nurse contracts rotate every 13 weeks. That's your tenant pipeline on autopilot.
- **Turnkey — real turnkey.** New HVAC, water heater, windows, kitchen cabinets with butcher block, sealed original hardwood floors. The flipper did the capex items you'd otherwise be replacing in years 1-3.
- **Seller is motivated.** LLC-owned, $14,900 price reduction, vacant, concessions offered. Agent remarks: "may be open to more work prior to closing for the right price."
- **No HOA.** No restrictions on furnished rentals. No tenant approval process. No monthly fee eating your cash flow.
- **FHA/VA eligible.** Widens your exit strategy if you need to sell to an owner-occupant.

### THE UGLY (Scars and All)

- **772 sqft, 2/1.** Your tenant pool is singles and couples. Period. If hospital demand softens, you're competing with 3/2s at $1,200/mo with 500 fewer square feet.
- **$187.82/sqft is steep for South Temple.** Non-renovated comps on this block trade at $80-100K. You're paying a premium for the flip. If the MTR doesn't work, your basis is high for the neighborhood.
- **Pier and beam, 1952 — 74 years old.** Pier and beam is inspectable (you can get under the house), but check every pier, every joist, and the subfloor. Moisture damage, settling, and deteriorated supports are common at this age.
- **Previous contract fell through.** I had clients under contract on this property. The inspection flagged items. Listing agent says they've been addressed. "Listing agent says" ≠ "your inspector verified." Get your own inspection.
- **Temple ISD, not Belton ISD.** Doesn't matter for MTR tenants (traveling nurses don't have local school-age kids). But it limits your resale and LTR appeal compared to Belton ISD properties at the same price point.
- **Your thesis is concentration risk.** The entire play depends on BSW/VA hospital demand for furnished housing. One variable. If that demand dries up, everything about this deal changes.

### TAYLOR'S VERDICT: Conditional Buy — MTR Only

Here's the deal on 1807 S 11th.

Don't buy this as a long-term rental. At $145K and $1,200/mo rent, you're writing a check every month to be a landlord. DSCR of 0.76 means you're feeding this thing $188/mo out of pocket. That's not investing, that's donating.

The mid-term rental play is where this property earns its price. Furnished at $1,800/mo, self-managed, you're cash flowing $479/mo. That's a 17.2% cash-on-cash on $33K invested. The tenant pipeline — traveling nurses cycling through BSW every 13 weeks — is real and renewable. I've watched the demand firsthand.

I'd negotiate. The flipper listed at $159.9K and already cut to $145K. They're sitting vacant and offering concessions. I'd come in at $130-135K with a 14-day close and inspection contingency. At $130K, your MTR cash-on-cash jumps to 22%+ and your LTR scenario gets closer to breakeven as a fallback.

One more thing: I've been inside this house with previous clients. The remodel quality is legitimate. But I'd still get my own inspection, especially on the piers, the plumbing under the house, and the electrical panel. The three things flippers skip are the three things that cost you $15K after closing.
