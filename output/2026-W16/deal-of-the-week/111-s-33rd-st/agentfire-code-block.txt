<h2>Why Is a Hard Money Lender Selling This Property?</h2>

<p>If you're not familiar with the backstory, here's what happened. Someone bought this building with a hard money loan &mdash; a short-term, high-interest loan designed for flippers and developers who plan to renovate and either sell or refinance within 6&ndash;18 months. That borrower couldn't execute. Maybe the rehab scope ballooned. Maybe financing dried up. Either way, they defaulted on the loan, and the hard money lender foreclosed.</p>

<p>Now the lender owns a 10,500 square foot vacant building that's generating zero income and costing them in carrying costs every month. They're not in the business of owning real estate &mdash; they're in the business of lending. They want this off their books.</p>

<p>That's the opportunity. Motivated sellers with no emotional attachment to the property. Their calculus is simple: recover as much principal as possible and move on. There's room to negotiate here, especially if you can close quickly with proof of funds.</p>

<p>One critical note: when a hard money lender forecloses and resells, title work is non-negotiable. You need a thorough title search for any liens, mechanic's liens from unpaid contractors, or secondary encumbrances the original borrower may have created. Don't skip this.</p>

Boarded windows, cast iron plumbing exposed in the ceilings, electrical panels from an era when "code" meant something different. But the bones are there &mdash; load-bearing walls are intact, the footprint is massive, and the existing unit layout (23 bedrooms across multiple attached and detached sections) already suggests a multi-unit conversion.</p>

<p>Here's what a full gut to 15 individual mid-term rental units looks like in hard dollars:</p>

<p>The ceiling height issue is the wild card most people won't anticipate. Several sections have 6.5 to 7.5 foot ceilings. For a mid-term rental targeting medical professionals paying $1,200 a month, you need livable ceiling heights. Raising them means modifying load-bearing structures &mdash; that's structural engineering, not drywall work.</p>

<h2>The Risks &mdash; Every One of Them</h2>

<p>I don't sanitize properties. The video walkthrough shows exactly how rough this building is, and you should watch it before making any decisions. Here's what keeps me up at night on this deal:</p>

<div class="dotw-risk">
<p><strong>1. Foundation is unknown.</strong> No inspection data exists for this building in its current state. On a 1948 structure this size, a structural inspection isn't optional &mdash; it's the first check you write before submitting an offer. Budget $2,000&ndash;$5,000 for the inspection alone, and keep $25,000 in contingency for what it finds.</p>
</div>

<div class="dotw-risk">
<p><strong>2. Asbestos is probable.</strong> 1948 construction means high probability of asbestos in popcorn ceilings, pipe insulation, and floor tiles. Professional abatement could add $20,000 to $40,000 to the rehab budget. You won't know until you test, and you can't demo until you know.</p>
</div>

<div class="dotw-risk">
<p><strong>3. Low ceilings are structural, not cosmetic.</strong> Some sections sit at 6.5 to 7.5 feet. Raising ceiling height in a building with load-bearing walls means engineering plans, structural modifications, and significant cost. This isn't a weekend project.</p>
</div>

<div class="dotw-risk">
<p><strong>4. Zoning and permits are unconfirmed.</strong> Converting a former assisted living center to 15 individual mid-term rental units requires City of Temple approval. Zoning confirmation for multi-unit MTR use is a prerequisite, not an afterthought. Get this answer before you close.</p>
</div>

<div class="dotw-risk">
<p><strong>5. Hard money foreclosure = title risk.</strong> The previous owner may have unpaid contractors, secondary liens, or other encumbrances. A thorough title search with lien waivers from any known contractors is mandatory.</p>
</div>

<div class="dotw-risk">
<p><strong>6. Zero income for 8&ndash;14 months.</strong> Full gut rehab on 10,500 SF doesn't happen in 90 days. You're carrying $1.2 million in total basis for the better part of a year with no revenue. Your financing structure needs to account for that carrying cost.</p>
</div>

<div class="dotw-risk">
<p><strong>7. Cast iron plumbing is non-negotiable replacement.</strong> You can't patch 78-year-old cast iron. Full PEX replacement across 15 units with kitchenettes and bathrooms is a $115,000 line item, and it's not optional.</p>
</div>

<h2>The BSW Proximity Advantage &mdash; Why Location Saves This Deal</h2>

<p>Strip the BSW angle and this is a 10,500 SF gut job in a residential area with questionable economics. The location is the entire thesis.</p>

<p>Baylor Scott &amp; White Medical Center is Temple's economic anchor &mdash; over 12,000 employees, continuous demand for housing from rotating medical staff, and zero signs of contraction. Travel nurses on 13-week contracts need furnished housing within a short commute. They're not signing 12-month leases in apartment complexes across town. They want proximity, furniture, and a reasonable price.</p>

<p>At $1,200 per unit furnished, you undercut every competing MTR in the BSW corridor while still generating $112,320 in NOI. That pricing power exists because your per-unit basis ($80,000) is dramatically lower than buying 15 individual properties. The commercial conversion is the arbitrage.</p>
