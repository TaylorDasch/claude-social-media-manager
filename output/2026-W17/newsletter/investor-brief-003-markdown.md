## THE DEEP DIVE

### New Construction vs Older Homes: The Five Numbers That Decide the Fight

Most new-versus-old content is cheerleading for whichever one the author sells. This isn't. Here are the five numbers that actually matter, sourced from Q1 2026 data.

**1. Insurance Premium Gap: +33% to +53%**
National average: new construction ~$966/yr versus 10-year-old home ~$1,478/yr — a 53% premium on older properties. Texas runs 19%+ above the national baseline and is the fastest-rising insurance market in America. Modern electrical reduces fire claims 60%. Modern plumbing reduces water damage claims by a similar margin. This gap is widening, not shrinking.

**2. CapEx Reserve Requirement: 2-3x for older**
New multifamily: $250-450 per unit per year. Older Class C: $600-800 per unit per year. On a 15-unit building, that's $2,250-$5,250 in real cash going into a reserve account — not your pocket. This line doesn't show up in most back-of-napkin cap rate math, which is why a lot of "8% cap" old builds end up being 6% caps after reserves.

**3. Texas Cap Rate Inversion: 100-250 bps spread**
New Class A Texas multifamily: 4.5-5.5% cap (DFW 5.7%, Houston 6.5%, Austin 5.5%). Older Class B/C: 5.8-8%+. The market is paying you 100-250 basis points to take on older-property operational drag. Whether that's enough depends on your cost of capital and your tolerance for 2am plumbing calls.

**4. Maintenance Labor Inflation: +15-22% since 2022**
Major repair labor is up 15-22% since the early 2020s. Multifamily firms are running 18-22% over planned CapEx budgets on actual 2026 work. If you modeled an $8K roof in 2022 numbers, you're writing a $10K check today. Older properties eat this premium annually. New construction sidesteps it for ~10 years.

**5. The Texas Concession War: 6-12 weeks free rent**
Texas rents are down year-over-year. Austin, San Antonio, and Dallas are giving 6-12 weeks of free rent as concessions on new Class A. Older inventory doesn't compete on amenity, so it doesn't compete on concessions. The effective rent gap between new and old is narrower than stated rents suggest.

### Think of it as two completely different businesses

**Buying new construction is a *finance business*.** You're buying predictability, low management, long hold, cheap insurance, easy refinancing, strong exit comps. Your edge is your cost of capital and your willingness to accept a 4.5-5.5% cap in exchange for near-zero operational drag. You win if rates come down and appreciation hits.

**Buying old is a *fix-it business*.** You're buying a 200 basis point cap rate premium in exchange for absorbing Texas insurance inflation, 20%+ higher CapEx, hidden capex discoveries during rehab, and active management. Your edge is that you can actually execute — you can fix what's broken at what you said it would cost.

*Neither is better. They're different games for different players.*

### Play #3 — BSW Badge Co-Living

Forget individual units with kitchenettes. Rehab into 20 private bedrooms across 5 shared suites — four rooms per suite, shared kitchen and two baths. Market exclusively to verified BSW staff with a badge. Think dorm-meets-Airbnb, not apartments.

**Numbers:** 20 beds at $900/bed/mo all-inclusive (furnished, utilities, Wi-Fi, cleaning) = $18,000/mo gross. Rehab drops to ~$580K because you're not building 15 kitchens. All-in ~$969K. After 15% vacancy and 38% OpEx (utilities included kills margin), NOI lands at ~$113,800. **Cap on cost: 11.7%.**

**Why it works:** $900/bed is the cheapest badge-gated room in Temple. **Why it doesn't:** Heavier management — you're running a boutique hotel, not a rental. One bad tenant infects a 4-person suite.

### Play #4 — Hospital Master Lease

Pitch BSW's Graduate Medical Education office or the hospital's travel nurse staffing vendor directly: lease the entire building to BSW for $15K/mo, 5-year term, hospital handles tenant placement and furnishing.

**Numbers:** $180K/yr gross, rehab stays MTR-spec but simpler (~$700K), all-in ~$1.09M. Hospital pays interior costs, you carry taxes, insurance, and structural reserves ($42K). NOI ~$138,000. **Cap on cost: 12.7%.**

**Why it works:** Zero vacancy. Zero management. Single AAA-rated tenant. Hospital's budget pays timely. **Why it doesn't:** BSW is not currently running this program in Temple (Dallas and Waco do). You'd have to pitch, propose, and wait 6-12 months for a signed deal — if ever. Don't underwrite on this hope.

### Play #5 — Keep the Assisted Living License

The building already has assisted living zoning and a grandfathered bed count. Do $150-200K minimum rehab to bring it to life safety code + ADA + kitchen compliance, then lease to a licensed assisted living operator on a triple-net basis.

**Numbers:** Rehab $175K. All-in $564K. Typical AL operator NNN lease runs 8-10% of all-in — so $45K-56K/yr. Operator pays taxes, insurance, repairs. **Cap on cost: 8-10%.**

**Why it works:** Lowest capital outlay of any play. 10-15 year leases standard. 1948 buildings holding AL licenses are increasingly rare — aging codes are getting stricter. This is a license arbitrage play, not a real estate play. **Why it doesn't:** Finding an AL operator willing to lease a 1948 shell is a real hunt. Operator credit matters — small operators default. You're fully exposed to one counterparty.

### Play #6 — Demo + New Construction 6-Plex

Tear it down. Build a brand-new 6-plex — three 2-bed units averaging 1,100 SF each.

**Numbers:** Demo + site prep $45K. Build cost 6,600 SF at $155/SF Temple 2026 = $1.02M. Permits and soft costs 6% = $61K. Total all-in **$1.52M**. Rent $1,450/unit (new-build LTR premium) = $104,400 gross/yr. After 5% vacancy and 25% OpEx (minimal — everything new), NOI ~$73,100. **Cap on cost: 4.8%.**

**Why it works:** New construction = lowest management burden, cheapest insurance, highest appreciation, 100% bonus depreciation if still around at closing. **Why it doesn't:** 4.8% cap on $1.52M is the worst return in this matrix. You're paying a $300K+ premium over the MTR rehab play for the privilege of owning new. This is the "new construction tax" in 2026 Texas.

### Play #7 — Medical Office Commercial Conversion

The building is 3 minutes from BSW. Convert to Class B medical office — PT/rehab clinic, pediatric dental, chiropractor, urgent care satellite — under a triple-net commercial lease.

**Numbers:** Rehab ~$450K to Class B medical office spec. All-in ~$839K. NNN lease at $18/SF on 10,500 SF = $189K/yr. Tenant pays taxes, insurance, repairs — you carry ~$8K structural reserves. NOI ~$181K. **Cap on cost: 21.6%.**

**Why it works:** Commercial medical near BSW is a premium use. 10-15 year leases standard. 21% cap is real if you sign an anchor. **Why it doesn't:** "If you sign an anchor" is doing all the work in that sentence. Commercial vacancy on a speculative medical office conversion can run 18-36 months before first signed lease. Carry $839K for 2 years at 7.25% DSCR = $122K burn before rent starts. This is the highest-ceiling play and the highest-risk play.
