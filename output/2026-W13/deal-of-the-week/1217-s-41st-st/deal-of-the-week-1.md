## What Makes This Deal Interesting

1. **$80,000 list price.** That is $145,000 below the 76504 median list. At this entry point, you're playing a different game than 90% of buyers in Temple.

2. **Direct rental comp on the same street.** 1706 S 41st — a 2/1 built in 1952, nearly identical to this property — is actively leased at $1,195/mo. That's not a projection. That's a real lease, right now, same block.

3. **1 mile from BSW.** The largest employer in Temple (8,884 employees). Residents, nurses, techs — all potential tenants. That proximity is a demand floor.

4. **South Temple odd-numbered streets.** This is the investor pocket. The area where buy-and-hold operators have been accumulating for years because the price-to-rent ratio works.

5. **Below the buy box floor.** Taylor's investor buy box is $140K-$260K. At $80K + $25K rehab = $105K all-in, this sits under the floor — meaning less competition from the typical Temple investor crowd.

---

## Scars and All — The Real Risks

1. **2/1 floor plan limits your tenant pool.** Most renters want at least 3 bedrooms. A 2/1 at 925 sqft is a singles/couples play, not families. That narrows demand.

2. **"As-is" means unknown unknowns.** Foundation on South Temple clay soil is a real concern. Plumbing in a 1950s home could be cast iron. HVAC may need full replacement. The $25K rehab estimate is optimistic — could easily run $35K-$40K if foundation or plumbing surprises show up.

3. **Financed, this is a break-even deal at best.** At 7.25% investor rates, the DSCR is 0.99 — basically washing. You're betting on appreciation and rent growth, not cash flow from day one.

4. **76504 appreciation is slower than 76502.** The north side of Temple (Canyon Creek, Bella Terra, Legacy Ranch) is where the growth catalysts are — Rowan, Meta, new builds. South Temple is stable, not surging.

5. **Older home = higher maintenance drag.** A 70-year-old property will have more capex events than a 2019 build. Budget accordingly.

## Who Is This Deal For?

**Cash buyers** looking to park capital at a 6.8% cap rate with a sub-$110K all-in basis. If you're comparing this to a 4.5% treasury or a 5% CD, the spread is there — plus you get the asset.

**BRRRR operators** — buy at $80K, rehab for $25-30K, rent at $1,195, refi at 75% of ARV. If the ARV appraises at $130K+ (14 S 41st sold for $165K as a 3/2 after full reno), you could pull most of your cash back.

**NOT for:** Leveraged cash-flow seekers expecting $200+/mo from day one. The math doesn't support that at current rates on a 2/1.

## Taylor's Take

Here's the deal on 1217 S 41st.

$80,000 for a 2/1 in South Temple, one mile from BSW. The rental comp — literally on the same street — is leased at $1,195 a month. That part checks out.

But let me be straight with you: financed at today's rates, this is a break-even play. DSCR is 0.99. You're not cash-flowing month one unless you're buying in cash.

The real play here is basis. $80K purchase, $25-30K rehab, all-in at $105-110K on a property where a renovated 3/2 a few doors down sold for $165K. If you're a BRRRR operator who can execute a clean rehab and refi, the spread is interesting.

What I'd want to know before writing an offer: foundation condition (expansive clay soil in this area — get the engineer out), plumbing material (cast iron vs. PEX), and the actual condition of the electrical panel. Those three items are the difference between a $25K rehab and a $45K rehab.

South Temple's odd-numbered streets have been an investor pocket for years, and the proximity to BSW gives you a built-in tenant pipeline. But I'd be lying if I said this was a slam dunk. It's a deal that rewards execution — if you nail the rehab budget, you're in great shape. If you miss it by $20K, the returns compress to where you might as well have bought a CD.

Would I buy this? At $80K with cash, yes — the cap rate works at 6.8% and I'd be under $115/sqft all-in. Financed? I'd want it at $65-70K to create enough margin for cash flow after debt service.
