## SECTION 3: AUDIENCE TARGETING (META ADS MANAGER SPECIFICS)

### Critical Compliance Note Before Targeting

Every ad you run promoting home purchases, rentals, buyer agent services, or real estate listings in the US must be declared under **Special Ad Category: Housing** in Meta Ads Manager. This is not optional. Failing to declare it risks ad rejection, reduced delivery, or account restriction. Declaring it imposes these restrictions:

- **Age:** Locked at 18-65+. Cannot narrow.
- **Gender:** All genders must be included. Cannot exclude.
- **Location:** Minimum 15-mile radius in the US. Cannot target by ZIP code. Cannot exclude locations.
- **Interest/behavior targeting:** Severely restricted. Many demographic categories become grayed out.
- **Standard Lookalike Audiences:** Not available in Special Ad Category Housing (Meta restricted these following a 2022 FTC settlement).
- **Texas-specific add-on:** Your ads must display the EG Realty broker name at font size at least half as large as your agent name. Include TREC license number in ad footer or landing page footer.

**The workaround that changes everything:**

Educational/lifestyle content ads about "living in Temple, TX" — not promoting home sales, not advertising agent services — do NOT trigger Special Ad Category. They operate as standard Meta ads with full targeting capabilities.

**Two-Stage Strategy:**
- **Stage 1** (no SAC restriction): Run "Temple, TX Living" video content as Traffic or Video Views campaigns. Full targeting — healthcare interests, military interests, behaviors, age, income proxy. Build a warm audience of engaged viewers.
- **Stage 2** (SAC applies, but audience is pre-warmed): Retarget Stage 1 video viewers with housing-specific lead gen ads. The SAC targeting restrictions apply, but you're showing housing ads to people who already demonstrated interest in Temple — dramatically higher conversion rates than cold housing SAC campaigns.

This two-stage approach is your biggest structural advantage over every other Temple agent running Meta Ads. They're all sending cold traffic into SAC-restricted campaigns and wondering why it's expensive.

### Statistical Significance in a Low-Volume Tertiary Market

This is where most tertiary market agents make bad decisions. They see 3 leads on one ad and 1 lead on another and conclude the first one "won." That's not data.

The minimum threshold for declaring a winner in Temple's volume context:
- Wait until each variant has generated **at least 25-30 leads** before comparing
- At $40/day budget and $50-70 CPL, that's 28-42 days per variant
- Use 90% confidence threshold (not the typical 95%) given the volume constraints
- Tools: Meta's built-in A/B test statistical significance calculator; Splitbase.io for a manual check

**Practical rule:** Do not kill an ad set before it has spent $300 and generated 10 leads. Do not declare a winner before 25 leads per variant. This is non-negotiable in a market this size.

**The exception:** If an ad spends $400 with a CTR of 0.2% and zero leads, kill it. Sometimes an ad just doesn't connect and no amount of patience changes that.

---

### Creative Refresh Cadence

**The signal to watch: Frequency.**

When your campaign frequency (average number of times each person has seen your ad) exceeds 2.5 in a 7-day window, creative fatigue is setting in. In a tertiary market with a smaller audience pool, you will hit 2.5 frequency faster than a DFW campaign would — expect 3-4 weeks per creative before fatigue, not 6-8 weeks.

**Creative pipeline rule:** Always have the next 2 ad variants ready to launch before the current ones show fatigue. Don't scramble for new creative when frequency hits 2.5 — you'll make rushed decisions.

**Refresh schedule (based on Temple's audience size):**
- Weeks 1-4: Original creative
- Week 5: Launch Variant B (new hook/image) alongside, begin A/B observation
- Week 7: Kill loser, keep winner, develop Variant C
- Week 9: Launch Variant C on the winner format
- Continue in 4-week cycles

**What to swap first when refreshing:** The hook/first frame. The headline. The CTA copy (different lead magnet angle). These have the highest impact on reducing fatigue and require the least production time.

**What not to swap first:** The audience targeting (stable, let it optimize), the campaign budget (leave it alone during creative testing), and the landing page (isolate your variables).

## SECTION 9: RISK, FAILURE MODES & CONTINGENCY

### The 5 Most Common Ways Solo Agents Burn Meta Money in Tertiary Markets

**1. Sending paid traffic to their homepage or IDX search page.**
The result: $50/day in spend, 1,000 impressions, 8 clicks, 0 leads, agent concludes "Meta doesn't work for real estate." Meta works fine. The destination was wrong. A homepage with navigation sends the user into a decision tree. A focused landing page with one CTA converts.

**2. Not declaring Special Ad Category: Housing.**
The result: Ads run fine for 2-3 days, then Meta's AI flags them as housing-related and restricts delivery or rejects. Account gets a policy strike. Recovery takes 7-30 days. Always declare upfront.

**3. Changing campaigns every 5 days.**
The result: The algorithm never learns. CPL remains high. Agent concludes Meta is too expensive. The algorithm needs 50 conversion events/week to optimize — in Temple, you'll be in learning mode for 4-6 weeks. Touching the campaign early resets the clock.

**4. Not having a follow-up system before the first dollar is spent.**
The result: Leads come in at 2am (or while you're in a listing appointment). You respond 3 hours later. Industry data shows a 100x drop in contact rate from 5 minutes to 30 minutes. The lead went to your competitor's site in the meantime. FUB auto-text eliminates this — but only if it's set up before launch.

**5. Evaluating Meta Ads on a 30-day return.**
The result: No closings at day 30 (normal), agent pauses campaign, restarts 60 days later with a new campaign (restarts learning phase), evaluates again at 30 days (again no closing), pauses again. This cycle repeats until the agent has spent $4,000 in 8 months with 3 learning phases and no sustained optimization. Real estate paid social requires a 90-180 day minimum evaluation window. Commit to it or don't start.

### Special Ad Category Housing Rules — What Gets Accounts Shut Down

**Actions that get accounts restricted or shut down:**

- Running a real estate agent service ad without declaring Special Ad Category: Housing
- Using ZIP code targeting in a housing campaign
- Using age exclusions in a housing campaign (e.g., targeting only 35-55)
- Using gender exclusions in a housing campaign
- Creating ads with language that implies restriction by protected class ("perfect for young professionals," "quiet neighborhood for retirees," "great school district families" — the last one is particularly risky as "familial status" is a protected class under the Fair Housing Act)
- Running standard Lookalike Audiences in a housing Special Ad campaign
- Excluding geographic areas in a housing campaign

**Actions that are compliant and often confused for violations:**

- Running Stage 1 (non-housing) awareness campaigns with full targeting — this is fine
- Targeting military interest categories in a Stage 1 campaign — fine
- Targeting healthcare interests in a Stage 1 campaign — fine
- Using income-proxy targeting in a Stage 1 campaign — fine
- Retargeting your FUB contact list as a Custom Audience — fine (these are your own customers)
- Using Advantage+ Audience in a Housing SAC campaign — fine

**Texas-specific compliance (beyond Meta):**

Under Texas Real Estate Commission rules, all real estate advertising must prominently display the broker's name. In Meta ads, include "EG Realty" in the body copy or description. In landing pages, include "Taylor Dasch | EG Realty | TREC License #[number]" in the footer.

Texas SB 140 (effective September 1, 2025): If any Meta lead is followed up by text message, that SMS communication falls under telemarketing law requirements. You need to register with the state and post a $10,000 bond if using SMS as a marketing tool. **The FUB auto-text to new leads may trigger this.** Consult a Texas real estate attorney on whether your specific FUB text workflow requires SB 140 registration. Note: The auto-text response to a form submission (where the lead has already opted in to contact by submitting their information) may have different classification than outbound cold SMS — but get a legal opinion before scaling.

### Diagnostic Tree: Month 1, Zero Qualified Leads

If you reach Day 30 with leads but zero qualified, run through this tree:

```
Q1: What is your CPM? 
├── > $40: Audience pool too small OR bid strategy issue. Try switching from "Lowest Cost" to "Bid Cap" at $30 target.
└── $20-$40: CPM is normal. Move to Q2.

Q2: What is your CTR?
├── < 0.5%: Creative/hook problem. Kill the ad and launch a new hook variant immediately (Section 5).
└── > 0.5%: Creative is working. Move to Q3.

Q3: What is your landing page conversion rate? (Leads ÷ Clicks × 100)
├── < 2%: Landing page friction. Shorten the form to name + email only. Move CTA above the fold. Check mobile load speed.
└── > 2%: Landing page is performing. Move to Q4.

Q4: What is your lead quality score average (FUB rubric)?
├── Avg < 3: Targeting mismatch. The ad is attracting informational browsers, not buyers. Revisit hook angle — are you offering too much "information" and not enough "decision tool"?
└── Avg 3-5: Quality is marginal but not broken. Move to Q5.

Q5: What is your response speed to leads?
├── > 15 min average: This is your #1 problem. Set up FUB auto-text. No other fix matters until response speed is under 5 minutes.
└── < 5 min: Speed is fine. Move to Q6.

Q6: Have you had any actual conversations with leads?
├── No: Leads are coming in but no one responds. Review your auto-text — is it actually sending? Check phone numbers for formatting issues.
└── Yes, but no appointment: The conversation is failing. Record your next 3 calls. Are you pitching too early? Are you asking about their situation before talking about listings?
```

---

### Diagnostic Tree: Leads Come In, None Convert to Appointments

```
Q1: Are you making contact with >25% of your leads?
├── No: Fix response speed and auto-text first (see above). Appointment conversion is meaningless if contact rate is broken.
└── Yes: Move to Q2.

Q2: What are leads saying when they disengage?
├── "Just looking / researching" (>60% of leads): Your ad attracted too many informational browsers. Shift ad hooks toward more decision-stage language. "Your move date is set. Here's what you need to know before you offer." Move away from "guide" offers toward "personalized analysis" offers.
├── "Not ready yet": Normal for 40-50% of leads. These are not appointment-stage — they need nurture. Are they in a FUB sequence? Review action plan content quality.
└── Specific objection ("rates too high," "not sure about Temple"): These are real conversations. Address these objections directly in your next ad creative — pre-handle before they become disengagement triggers.

Q3: Is your appointment CTA clear and low-friction?
├── No: You may be asking for an in-person meeting on the first touchpoint. Replace with: "20-minute Zoom call, no obligation, I'll answer your top 3 questions about [neighborhood/process]."
└── Yes: Move to Q4.

Q4: Is your lead magnet creating an expectation mismatch?
├── Example: You offered a "relocation guide" (information seeker hook) but your follow-up goes straight to "let's talk listings" (buyer agent pitch). 
└── Fix: Your follow-up sequence should deliver on the lead magnet promise first (send the guide, talk about what's in it) before pivoting to appointment. The appointment ask should come on Day 5-7, not Day 1.
```

### When to Pause vs. Push Through

**Pause the program when:**
- Your Meta account receives a policy violation strike (resolve it before spending more)
- Your landing page is down or generating errors (check weekly)
- Your FUB auto-text is not firing (leads are going cold; fix the system before wasting more spend)
- You've received more than 25 leads but your contact rate is below 10% (systemic follow-up failure; fix CRM before generating more volume)
- You are personally at capacity with buyer clients and cannot handle more (better to pause than to poison your conversion rates and waste money on leads you can't follow up)

**Push through when:**
- Zero closings at 90 days (normal, expected)
- CPL is higher than expected ($80-$100 range) but leads are arriving and quality is improving
- Several leads went dark after initial contact (normal conversion rates — 75-80% of real estate leads never transact)
- Your creative is fatiguing (normal — refresh creative without pausing the campaign)
